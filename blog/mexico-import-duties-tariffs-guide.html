How Mexico Import Duties Work (The Basics)

Mexico's import tax system has three core components that apply to every commercial shipment crossing the border. Understanding all three is the difference between an accurate cost estimate and a nasty surprise at the port.

IGI: The Main Import Duty (0-35%)

The Impuesto General de Importacion (IGI) is Mexico's primary import tariff. Rates range from 0% to 35% depending on the product's tariff classification. Most consumer goods fall between 10% and 25%. Some categories, like textiles and footwear, sit at the top end. Electronics and industrial equipment often land at the lower end.

The IGI is calculated on the CIF value of your shipment: the product cost, plus insurance, plus international freight to the Mexican port of entry. Not FOB. Not the price on your commercial invoice alone. CIF. This distinction matters because it increases the base value your duty rate applies to.

IVA: The 16% Value-Added Tax

Every import into Mexico carries a 16% IVA (Impuesto al Valor Agregado), applied on the CIF value plus the IGI duty. So if your CIF value is $10,000 and your duty is $2,000, IVA applies to $12,000, not $10,000.

The critical detail: IVA is recoverable. If you import under your own Mexican entity with a registered RFC, the 16% becomes an input tax credit against IVA collected on your Mexican sales. It is not a sunk cost. But you can only recover it with proper monthly tax filings through SAT.

DTA: Customs Processing Fee (~0.8%)

The Derecho de Tramite Aduanero (DTA) is a customs processing fee of approximately 0.8% of the CIF value. It is a small line item, but on a $50,000 shipment, that is $400 you need to account for.

How the Math Works

Here is the formula for calculating your total import taxes on any shipment to Mexico:

Component Rate Base
IGI (Import Duty) 0-35% CIF value
IVA (Value-Added Tax) 16% CIF + IGI
DTA (Processing Fee) ~0.8% CIF value
Total Tax Load Varies Sum of above

The 10-digit fraccion arancelaria determines your IGI rate. This is Mexico's tariff classification system, maintained by SAT and aligned with the international Harmonized System (HS) codes. Getting this number right is the single most important step in import cost planning. We will cover it in detail below.

The Hidden Costs Nobody Tells You About

The tables above cover the standard line items. But experienced importers know about the extras that add up fast:

  • Pre-validation fees: Mexican customs requires electronic pre-validation of your customs declaration. Fee: $20-40 per declaration.
  • Red-light inspection: Random physical inspections at the port. If selected, expect 1-3 days of additional storage fees ($50-150/day) and inspection handling ($100-200).
  • Demurrage and detention: If your paperwork is not ready when the container arrives, port charges accumulate quickly. Container detention runs $75-150/day after the free period.
  • FX conversion: Duties are assessed in MXN. If your accounting is in USD, you face conversion costs and timing risk on volatile days.
  • Padron Sectorial requirements: Certain product categories (electronics, textiles, chemicals, footwear) require additional Padron Sectorial registration beyond the standard Padron de Importadores. Missing this can freeze your shipment.

Understanding Your Tariff Code (Fraccion Arancelaria)

Your fraccion arancelaria is a 10-digit code that determines everything: your duty rate, whether you need a Padron Sectorial, which regulatory permits are required, and whether your product qualifies for USMCA preferential rates. Get this wrong and you face overpaid duties, fines, or seizure.

What the 10-Digit Code Means

Mexico's tariff classification system is built on the international Harmonized System (HS). The first 6 digits are universal across countries. Digits 7-8 are specific to the Latin American trade region (NALADI/NALADISA). Digits 9-10 are Mexico's own further subdivision.

For example, a collagen supplement might look like this:

  • 2106 - Food preparations not elsewhere specified (HS Chapter 21)
  • 2106.90 - Other food preparations
  • 2106.90.99 - Regional subdivision for "other"
  • 2106.90.99.01 - Mexico-specific: dietary supplement preparations

The difference between 2106.90.99.01 (15% duty) and a misclassification under 3004.90.99.99 (pharmaceutical preparation, 0% duty but requiring full pharma registration) is the difference between a smooth import and a six-month customs hold.

Why Misclassification Happens

Amazon sellers misclassify products for three common reasons:

  1. Using their US HS code directly. The first 6 digits may match, but Mexico's 10-digit code assigns different duty rates. A product at 5% in the US might sit at 20% in Mexico.
  2. Optimistic classification. Choosing a code with a lower duty rate without verifying the product actually fits that classification. Customs audits catch this, and fines run 70-100% of the duty difference.
  3. Ignoring product composition. Mexico classifies based on the primary material or active ingredient. A "yoga mat" made of PVC classifies differently than one made of natural rubber, and the duty rates may differ by 10+ percentage points.

Tally coordinates with vetted customs brokers who verify tariff classification before your shipment leaves origin. This is not a step you skip.

Sectoral Padron Requirements

Beyond the standard Padron de Importadores (required for all imports), certain fraccion arancelaria codes trigger the need for a Padron de Importadores de Sectores Especificos. Product categories that require sectoral registration include:

  • Sector 10: Textiles and apparel
  • Sector 11: Footwear
  • Sector 14: Iron and steel products
  • Sector 15: Products from the chemical industry
  • Sector 16: Electronics and electrical equipment

Importing goods that require a Padron Sectorial without having it registered will result in your shipment being detained at the port. Registration is done through SAT and typically takes 5-10 business days once your entity and RFC are active.

USMCA: How US and Canadian Sellers Pay Less (or Zero) on Mexico Import Duties

If your products are manufactured or substantially transformed in the United States or Canada, the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA) can reduce your IGI duty rate to 0%. This is the single biggest cost advantage US and Canadian sellers have over competitors shipping from China, Korea, or elsewhere.

Consider this: while a Chinese competitor pays 15-25% IGI on the same product, a USMCA-qualifying US brand pays 0%. On a $500,000 annual import volume, that is $75,000-$125,000 in annual savings. That margin advantage compounds with every shipment.

What Qualifies for USMCA Preferential Rates

Not every US-origin product automatically qualifies. The product must meet the rules of origin specific to its tariff heading. Generally, this means one of the following:

  • Wholly obtained: The product was entirely grown, mined, or produced in the US/Canada/Mexico (agricultural products, raw materials).
  • Substantial transformation: The product was manufactured or processed enough in the US/Canada that the tariff classification changed (the "tariff shift" rule).
  • Regional Value Content (RVC): A minimum percentage of the product's value (typically 50-75% depending on the method) originates from North America.

Certificate of Origin Requirements

To claim USMCA preferential rates, you need a valid Certificate of Origin. Under USMCA, this does not require a specific government-issued form. The exporter, producer, or importer can self-certify using a declaration that includes:

  • Certifier name and contact information
  • Exporter and producer information (if different from the certifier)
  • Importer information
  • Description and HS tariff classification of the goods
  • Origin criterion met (tariff shift, RVC, wholly obtained)
  • Blanket period (certificates can cover up to 12 months of shipments)

Keep documentation proving your origin claim for a minimum of five years. Mexican customs can audit USMCA claims retroactively, and if you cannot produce the records, you owe the full duty plus penalties.

The 19% Courier Surcharge: What Chinese Sellers Must Know

If you are a Chinese seller shipping products to Mexico through courier services like DHL, FedEx, or UPS for commercial resale, you are likely paying an additional surcharge of up to 19% on top of your standard import duties. This surcharge applies to goods imported via express courier that exceed de minimis thresholds and are destined for commercial sale.

What Triggers the Surcharge

The surcharge kicks in when:

  • Goods are imported via courier or express parcel services (not formal ocean/air freight)
  • The shipment exceeds de minimis value thresholds ($50 USD for Mexico)
  • Goods are intended for commercial resale, not personal use
  • The importer does not have a Mexican entity or Padron de Importadores

Many Chinese Amazon sellers fall into this trap because they ship inventory to Mexico using the same courier-based logistics they use for the US. In the US, this works. In Mexico, it is a margin killer.

How to Eliminate It: Formal IOR Import

The solution is straightforward: form a Mexican entity, register your Padron de Importadores, and import formally through a licensed customs broker under your own Importer of Record (IOR) status.

VAT Recovery: Getting Your 16% Back

The 16% IVA on imports is the largest single tax component for most sellers. On a $50,000 shipment with 20% duty, IVA adds $9,600. But here is what separates experienced Mexico sellers from beginners: that $9,600 is not a cost if you set up correctly.

How IVA Recovery Works

IVA on imports functions as an input tax credit. When you sell products in Mexico, you collect IVA from your buyers (Amazon collects and remits this for marketplace sellers). The IVA you paid on imports offsets the IVA you owe on sales. If your import IVA exceeds your sales IVA in a given month, you accumulate a credit balance that carries forward.

The catch: you must import under your own Mexican entity with a registered RFC and active eFirma. If you import through a courier, through someone else's entity, or through a Merchant of Record, the IVA recovery goes to them, not you.

The Monthly Filing Process

  1. Import documentation. Your customs broker generates a pedimento (customs declaration) showing the IVA paid. This is your proof of input tax.
  2. Monthly tax return. Your accountant files the monthly IVA return with SAT, declaring both collected IVA (from sales) and creditable IVA (from imports and expenses).
  3. Net position. If collected IVA exceeds creditable IVA, you pay the difference. If creditable exceeds collected, the balance carries forward.
  4. Annual reconciliation. The annual tax return reconciles all monthly positions.

Proper Mexican tax compliance and accounting is not optional here. Without clean monthly filings, you cannot recover IVA, and 16% of every import becomes a permanent cost.

How IVA Recovery Improves Your Margins

Consider a seller importing $300,000 in products annually at a 15% IGI rate:

  • Annual IGI duty: $45,000 (non-recoverable)
  • Annual IVA: $55,200 (recoverable)
  • Without IVA recovery: Total tax load = $100,200 (33.4% of product value)
  • With IVA recovery: Effective tax load = $45,000 (15% of product value)

That $55,200 difference is the margin between a profitable Mexico operation and a marginal one. It is also why importing under your own entity matters far more than any short-term convenience of using someone else's import structure.

Customs Broker Fees and Other Import Costs

Beyond duties and taxes, every shipment into Mexico involves fees for the licensed professionals and infrastructure that move your goods from port to fulfillment center.

Customs Broker (Agente Aduanal)

Mexican law requires a licensed customs broker (agente aduanal) to process formal imports. You cannot clear customs yourself. Fees vary based on shipment complexity, value, and port of entry:

  • Standard shipment: $250-$400 per shipment
  • COFEPRIS-regulated products: $350-$600 per shipment (additional documentation)
  • First-time importers: May face higher fees as brokers set up your account and verify documentation
  • High-value shipments ($100K+): Fees may be calculated as a percentage of CIF (typically 0.3-0.5%)

Tally coordinates with vetted customs brokers who have experience with Amazon FBA imports specifically. This matters because FBA shipments have specific labeling, palletizing, and delivery appointment requirements that not all brokers handle efficiently.

Bonded Warehouse Fees

When your goods arrive at a Mexican port, they enter a bonded warehouse (almacen fiscal) until customs clearance is complete. Fees depend on the port and storage duration:

  • First 5-7 days: Often included in the port handling fee
  • After free period: $50-$150 per day depending on volume and port
  • Long-term storage (30+ days): Rates increase significantly, and goods may be declared abandoned after 90 days

Land Transport from Port to FBA

The cost of trucking your goods from the port of entry to Amazon's fulfillment centers varies by route:

Route Cost (Full Truckload) Transit Time
Laredo/Nuevo Laredo to CDMX FC $1,200-$1,800 2-3 days
Manzanillo to CDMX FC $800-$1,200 1-2 days
Lazaro Cardenas to CDMX FC $900-$1,400 1-2 days
Veracruz to CDMX FC $700-$1,100 1 day

For LTL (less-than-truckload) shipments, costs are proportionally higher per unit. Most Amazon sellers importing at commercial volume use full truckload or consolidated freight to minimize per-unit transport costs.

Other Costs to Budget

  • Pre-validation (previo): $20-$40 per customs declaration
  • Inspection handling: $100-$200 if your shipment is selected for physical inspection
  • Documentation preparation: $50-$100 for commercial invoice, packing list, and certificate of origin preparation
  • Insurance: 0.3-0.5% of shipment value (optional but recommended)

Common Import Mistakes That Cost Amazon Sellers Money

After coordinating imports for thousands of brands, these are the six mistakes Tally sees most often. Every one of them is avoidable with proper planning.

1. Wrong Tariff Classification

Using your US HS code without converting to the Mexican 10-digit fraccion arancelaria. The duty rate difference can be 10-20 percentage points, and misclassification fines run 70-100% of the duty shortfall. Always have a licensed customs broker verify your code before shipping.

2. Not Registering in Padron de Importadores

You cannot import commercially into Mexico without being registered in SAT's Padron de Importadores. This requires a Mexican entity with an active RFC and eFirma. Sellers who skip this step find their goods stuck at the port with no legal path to release them. Entity formation is the first step.

3. Using Courier for Commercial Volume

Courier imports work for samples and small test orders. For commercial volume, you pay premium shipping rates, risk the 19% surcharge, and lose IVA recovery eligibility. Any seller importing more than $5,000/month in product should switch to formal IOR import.

4. Not Recovering IVA Through Proper Accounting

The 16% IVA on every import is recoverable, but only with clean monthly tax filings through SAT. Sellers who delay setting up accounting or file incorrectly leave thousands of dollars on the table every quarter. Tally's plans include monthly accounting and tax compliance starting at $588/month.

5. Shipping COFEPRIS-Regulated Products Without Permits

Supplements, cosmetics, food and beverages, and medical devices all require COFEPRIS registration before import. Shipping regulated products without the proper Aviso de Funcionamiento or Registro Sanitario results in customs seizure, product destruction, or forced re-export at your expense.

6. Not Getting a Cost Estimate Before Shipping

This is the mistake that ties all the others together. Sellers who calculate their landed cost in advance can catch classification errors, budget for duties accurately, identify COFEPRIS requirements, and decide whether the Mexico margin works before committing $50,000+ in inventory. Sellers who guess discover problems when their container is sitting in a bonded warehouse at $150/day.

Frequently Asked Questions About Mexico Import Duties

What are the import duties for Mexico?

Mexico import duties (IGI) range from 0% to 35% depending on your product's 10-digit tariff classification (fraccion arancelaria). All imports also carry 16% IVA (recoverable) and a ~0.8% DTA customs processing fee. Duties are calculated on CIF value: product cost + insurance + freight.

Can USMCA reduce my Mexico import duties to zero?

Yes. Products manufactured or substantially transformed in the US or Canada can qualify for 0% duty under USMCA. You need a valid Certificate of Origin and your product must meet the rules of origin for its specific tariff heading. Merely transshipping through the US does not qualify.

How is IVA on imports recovered in Mexico?

The 16% IVA paid on imports is recoverable as an input tax credit against IVA collected on your Mexican sales. You must import under your own entity with a registered RFC, file monthly tax returns through SAT, and maintain proper CFDI documentation. Without clean accounting, you cannot recover it.

What is the 19% courier surcharge for Mexico imports?

Shipments entering Mexico via courier or express services for commercial resale face an additional surcharge of up to 19% on top of standard duties. Forming a Mexican entity and importing formally through a licensed customs broker eliminates this surcharge entirely.

How much does a customs broker cost in Mexico?

Licensed customs broker (agente aduanal) fees in Mexico typically range from $250 to $600 per shipment, depending on product complexity, shipment value, and port of entry. COFEPRIS-regulated products cost more due to additional documentation requirements.

Do I need a Padron de Importadores to import to Mexico?

Yes. The Padron de Importadores is Mexico's official import registry maintained by SAT. You need a Mexican entity with an active RFC and eFirma to register. Without it, your goods will be held at customs. Certain product categories also require a Padron Sectorial.

How long does Mexico customs clearance take?

With proper documentation and a registered Padron de Importadores, customs clearance takes 2-5 business days for standard shipments. Red-light inspections can add 1-3 days. First-time imports may take longer if documentation needs correction.

What happens if my tariff code is wrong on a Mexico import?

Wrong tariff classification can result in underpaid or overpaid duties, fines of 70-100% of the duty difference, seizure of goods, or a ban from the Padron de Importadores. Always have a licensed customs broker verify classification before your shipment leaves origin.

How do I estimate my total import cost for Mexico?

You need your product's HS code, country of origin, declared value, shipping method, and volume. Total landed cost = product cost + freight + insurance + duties (0-35%) + IVA (16%) + DTA (~0.8%) + broker fees + transport to FBA. Tally provides free import cost estimates.

What is the difference between T1 and IOR import in Mexico?

T1 is a test shipment via a partner warehouse using de minimis entry, designed for brands testing Mexico with a small first order. IOR (Importer of Record) is formal import under your own Mexican entity using your Padron de Importadores. IOR gives full IVA recovery and lower per-unit costs at scale.
