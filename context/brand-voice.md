# Tally Global Brand Voice & Messaging

This document defines the Tally Global brand voice, tone, and messaging framework. Reference this when writing all content to ensure consistency.

## Brand Voice Pillars

### 1. Expert Authority
- **What it means**: We have formed 4,500+ companies in Mexico across 20+ countries. We speak from direct, verifiable experience with Mexican regulations, customs, banking, and marketplace operations.
- **How it sounds**: Confident, specific, data-backed. We cite real timelines, real costs, and real regulatory references. No hedging, no "it depends" without explaining what it depends on.
- **Example**: "Entity formation takes 4-6 weeks. Banking runs in parallel at 2-4 weeks. Most brands go from zero to first sale in 8-12 weeks."
- **Avoid**: Vague claims ("we are experts"), unsupported assertions, hedging language ("this could potentially be helpful")

### 2. Direct and No BS
- **What it means**: We cut straight to what the reader needs to know. Every sentence earns its place. No filler paragraphs, no "in this article we will discuss," no corporate throat-clearing.
- **How it sounds**: Short paragraphs, specific numbers, clear structure. Lead with the answer, then support it.
- **Example**: "Since January 2025, SAT requires all Amazon sellers to have a legal representative in Mexico. No shortcuts."
- **Avoid**: Fluff intros, filler transitions ("without further ado"), wishy-washy language, anything that sounds like a brochure

### 3. Approachable Advisor
- **What it means**: "The friend who happens to be a Mexico business lawyer." We make complex regulatory topics understandable without dumbing them down. We explain the why, not just the what.
- **How it sounds**: Conversational but professional. We use "you" and "your." We explain acronyms the first time. We use analogies to bridge gaps (e.g., "S de RL de CV, the Mexican equivalent of a US LLC").
- **Example**: "COFEPRIS is Mexico's equivalent of the FDA. If your products touch the human body, you need COFEPRIS clearance before importing."
- **Avoid**: Jargon without context, condescending tone, overly casual language (no slang, no memes), academic dryness

### 4. Ownership-Focused
- **What it means**: Our core differentiator is that the client owns everything: the entity, the bank account, the import permits, the marketplace accounts. We hammer this home because it is what separates us from aggregators and MoR services.
- **How it sounds**: Empowering, assertive. We position ownership as the smart, long-term play versus renting access through intermediaries.
- **Example**: "They rent you access. We give you ownership. Your entity, your bank account, your import permits, your marketplace accounts."
- **Avoid**: Downplaying competitors without substance, making ownership sound complicated, suggesting that renting access is ever the right call for serious brands

### 5. Speed and Urgency
- **What it means**: The Mexico market is growing fast (34% YoY on Amazon, $54.4B ecommerce market). We convey urgency without being salesy. The data creates the urgency; we just present it clearly.
- **How it sounds**: Forward-looking, opportunity-driven. We use real market data to create FOMO based on facts, not pressure tactics.
- **Example**: "Mexico's ecommerce market hit $54.4 billion in 2025 and is projected to reach $175.8 billion by 2034. The window is wide open, but seller density is climbing."
- **Avoid**: Hype language, countdown timers, fake scarcity, "act now or miss out" pressure

## Tone Guidelines

### General Tone
Imagine you are an experienced Mexico market entry advisor helping a smart, busy founder figure out how to launch in Mexico. You know the regulatory landscape inside and out, you genuinely want them to succeed, and you explain things clearly without being patronizing. You are direct because their time is valuable. You are specific because vague advice is worthless.

### Tone Variations by Content Type

**How-To Guides**: Instructive, step-by-step, precise
- "Here is the realistic timeline. With Tally managing the process, most brands go from zero to first sale in 8-12 weeks."
- "Step 1: Entity Formation. Draft articles of incorporation, appoint legal representative, notarize with a Mexican notary public."
- "Non-compliant products will be seized at customs. Get your NOM labels right the first time."

**Strategy/Advice Content**: Analytical, data-driven, decisive
- "Every metric points in the same direction: Mexico is the fastest-growing ecommerce opportunity in the Western Hemisphere."
- "Less competition means lower advertising costs, stronger organic rankings, and healthier margins."
- "The infrastructure you build for Amazon Mexico transfers directly to Mercado Libre."

**Comparison/Competitive Content**: Factual, specific, contrast-driven
- "NocNoc is an aggregator. They sell your products under their entity, their listings, their brand. You own nothing."
- "Passport handles shipping. Shipping is not market entry. You need entity, banking, compliance."
- "DIY takes 12-18 months alone and costs 3x more. We do it in 8-12 weeks."

**Regulatory/Compliance Content**: Authoritative, precise, reassuring
- "COFEPRIS cosmetics notifications take 3-15 days. Supplement Registro Sanitario takes 3-6 months. Start early."
- "The 16% IVA charged on imports is recoverable as an input tax credit. It is not a sunk cost."

## Writing Style Guidelines

### Sentence Structure
- **Vary length**: Mix short declarative punches with longer explanatory sentences. Short sentences for emphasis. Longer ones for nuance and context.
- **Active voice preferred**: "Tally handles entity formation" not "Entity formation is handled by Tally"
- **Average length**: 12-20 words per sentence
- **Clarity first**: If a sentence requires rereading, rewrite it

### Paragraph Structure
- **Length**: 2-4 sentences typically. Maximum 3 paragraphs without a heading break.
- **One idea per paragraph**: Each paragraph makes one point and supports it
- **Transitions**: Data-driven or logical ("Here is why," "The result," "That means")
- **White space**: Generous. Content should feel open and scannable, not dense.

### Word Choice
- **Specific over generic**: "$588/month" not "affordable," "4-6 weeks" not "a few weeks," "4,500+ companies" not "many companies"
- **Active verbs**: "Launch," "Form," "Import," "Own," "Build," "Register"
- **Mexico-specific vocabulary**: Use correct Mexican terms (RFC, eFirma, CFDI, S de RL de CV, Padron de Importadores) with brief explanation on first use
- **Cut the filler**: Delete "very," "really," "actually," "basically," "just," "simply" unless they add meaning

### Terminology

**Say This** --> **Not That**
- "entity formation" --> "company registration" (more precise in Mexico context)
- "S de RL de CV" --> "Mexican LLC" (use proper name, then explain the analogy)
- "COFEPRIS" --> "Mexican FDA" (use real name, then explain the analogy)
- "8-12 weeks" --> "a few months" (always be specific)
- "$588/month" --> "affordable pricing" (always show the number)
- "4,500+ companies" --> "thousands of companies" (specificity builds trust)
- "own your entity" --> "we help you set up" (ownership language, not service language)
- "market entry" --> "expansion" (market entry is the correct frame for Mexico)

## Legal Language Rules (from Terms & Conditions)

Tally is a **coordinator and advisor**, not a direct service provider. All operational services (warehousing, customs clearance, importing) are performed by licensed third-party partners. This is critical for legal/regulatory compliance.

- NEVER say "our warehouse" or "Tally's warehouse" — we use partner facilities
- NEVER say "we import" or "Tally imports" — we coordinate imports through licensed partners
- NEVER say "we clear customs" — licensed customs brokers do this, we coordinate
- NEVER say "Tally handles customs clearance" — say "Tally coordinates with licensed customs brokers"
- ALWAYS include the disclaimer: Tally is a coordinator/advisor, not the direct service provider
- ALWAYS say "partner warehouse", "partner network", "vetted customs brokers"
- OK to say: "we coordinate", "we manage the process", "we connect you with", "through our partner network"

These phrases are fine and should NOT be changed:
- "your warehouse" (client's own warehouse)
- "Amazon's warehouses" / "FBA warehouses" (Amazon's facilities)
- "bonded warehouse" (customs term, not Tally's property)
- "Warehouse & Handling" in cost tables (general import cost category)

## What We Never Say

- "Click here" or "Read more" (use descriptive anchor text)
- "In this article, we will discuss..." (just start discussing it)
- "Without further ado..." (fluff)
- "It depends" without explaining what it depends on
- "Best in class" / "world class" / "cutting edge" (empty superlatives)
- "Solutions" as a standalone noun (what does it mean?)
- "Leverage" as a verb (overused corporate speak)
- "Affordable" without a number attached
- "Contact us for more information" (give them the information)
- "Easy" without proving it with specifics

## Competitor Positioning

### vs NocNoc (Aggregator)
- **Their model**: They sell your products under their entity on Amazon Mexico. You do not own the listing, the account, or the entity.
- **Our line**: "They rent you access. We give you ownership."
- **When to mention**: When discussing marketplace entry options, when reader asks about "easier" alternatives
- **Key differentiator**: Ownership, margins, brand control, exit value

### vs Passport (Shipping/Logistics)
- **Their model**: Cross-border shipping and logistics. No entity formation, no compliance, no banking.
- **Our line**: "Shipping is not market entry. You need entity, banking, compliance."
- **When to mention**: When discussing cross-border logistics, when reader thinks shipping alone is sufficient
- **Key differentiator**: Comprehensive market entry vs. single-function logistics

### vs Outpost (Merchant of Record)
- **Their model**: They act as the importer/seller of record. You do not own the Mexican entity or assets.
- **Our line**: "MoR means you own nothing. Tally means you own everything."
- **When to mention**: When discussing import structures, when reader evaluates MoR vs. owned entity
- **Key differentiator**: Asset ownership, long-term value, independence

### vs DIY (Doing It Yourself)
- **Their model**: Hiring individual lawyers, accountants, customs brokers, and navigating Mexican bureaucracy independently.
- **Our line**: "Takes 12-18 months alone, costs 3x more. We do it in 8-12 weeks."
- **When to mention**: When reader considers going it alone, when discussing timeline and cost
- **Key differentiator**: Speed, cost, single point of contact, proven process

**Primary Audience**: International brand founders and ecommerce operators evaluating Mexico market entry
- Already selling successfully in their home market (usually US or China)
- Revenue $1M-$50M, growth-stage
- Sophisticated enough to understand entity structures and compliance requirements
- Time-constrained, want to move fast
- Risk-aware, need to trust the process

**Secondary Audiences**:
- **Amazon aggregator employees**: Evaluating Mexico expansion for portfolio brands
- **Ecommerce consultants/agencies**: Advising clients on Mexico strategy
- **VC/PE professionals**: Conducting due diligence on LatAm expansion plays

### What They Care About
1. How long will it take? (Timeline)
2. How much will it cost? (Budget)
3. What do I actually own? (Ownership structure)
4. What can go wrong? (Risk mitigation)
5. Who has done this before? (Social proof)

### Pain Points
1. Mexico regulatory complexity feels overwhelming
2. Fear of getting stuck in bureaucracy for months
3. Do not know who to trust (bad experiences with local lawyers)
4. Worried about losing control of their brand/IP
5. Unsure if the Mexico market is worth the investment
